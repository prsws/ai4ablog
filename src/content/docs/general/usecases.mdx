### 1. Memory Recall (The Core Function)

#### Scenario: “Where did I put that?”

You vaguely remember storing an important document but can’t recall where.

You ask Pepa:

> “Pepa, where is the warranty for the water heater?”
>

Pepa searches across:

- scanned and indexed documents
- stored emails
- notes

Then responds:

> “The water heater warranty was scanned on March 3rd and stored in Paperless under Home Appliances.”
>

It can also:

- open the document
- show the location
- summarize its key details.

And when Pepa remembers something *wrong*, you simply say so:

> “Pepa, that’s not right — I keep the warranties in the file cabinet now.”
>

Your word always wins. **You are the authority on your own life — Pepa’s memory serves you, never the other way around.**

### 2. Life Context Recall

#### Scenario: “What did the doctor say last time?”

You can’t remember the details from a previous appointment.

You ask:

> “Pepa, what did the doctor recommend about my blood pressure medication?”
>

Pepa searches:

- appointment notes
- documents
- messages
- health logs

Then summarizes:

> “At your January appointment Dr. Rivera recommended continuing Losartan and monitoring blood pressure twice daily.”
>

The goal is **contextual recall**, not just document search.

### 3. Daily Task Assistance

#### Scenario: “What should I be doing today?”

You ask:

> “Pepa, what’s on my radar today?”
>

Pepa compiles information from:

- calendar
- reminders
- home maintenance schedules
- medication reminders
- ongoing projects

Response example:

> “Today you planned to replace the UPS battery in the rack, review the NAS backup logs, and call the pharmacy.”
>

This is **life context aggregation**.

### 4. Voice & Home Control — In Your Own Language

#### Scenario: “Apaga el abanico, por favor.”

You speak to Pepa the way you speak at home — English, español, or the Spanglish in between:

> “Pepa, apaga el abanico de la oficina.”
>

> “Pepa, turn on the porch light.”
>

Pepa hears you, understands you, and does it — controlling lights, fans, air conditioners, and doors throughout the house. From the sofa, from the kitchen, even from the car on the way home.

And here is the part that matters: **your voice never leaves your house.** No cloud service listens in, no company stores your words, no subscription decides whether your lights work today.

Aging with dignity includes being understood **in your own language, on your own terms**. This one is not a promise — it works today.

### 5. Knowledge Management

#### Scenario: remembering useful information from months — or years — ago.

You ask:

> “Pepa, what was that article about AI memory systems I saved?”
>

Or:

> “Pepa, how did I fix the UPS monitoring issue last time?”
>

Pepa searches your article queue, saved links, troubleshooting notes, and past solutions, then replies:

> “You saved an article on memory architectures for GenAI systems — it discussed separating episodic from semantic memory. And you solved the UPS issue by proxying SNMP through the Raspberry Pi NUT server.”
>

Over the years this grows into a **personal knowledge archive**: documents, notes, decisions, mistakes, and lessons — retrievable long after you would otherwise have forgotten they existed. It prevents **re-solving the same problem repeatedly**, and it preserves what you’ve learned for as long as you need it.

### 6. Technical Homelab Assistance

#### Scenario: troubleshooting your own infrastructure.

You ask:

> “Pepa, why did the NAS reboot yesterday?”
>

Pepa checks:

- system logs
- UPS status
- monitoring alerts
- network events

Then summarizes:

> “The TerraMaster NAS shut down after a brief power outage at 2:13 AM. The UPS battery reached low capacity.”
>

This turns Pepa into a **system memory for the lab itself**.

---

### 7. Pattern Detection Over Time

#### Scenario: noticing something you might miss.

Pepa periodically analyzes stored data and alerts you:

> “Your UPS battery capacity has dropped 15% over the last three months.”
>

Or:

> “Your sleep log shows increasing irregularity over the past two weeks.”
>

One important restraint: Pepa reports **what it observed, never a diagnosis**. “Your resting heart rate has been higher this week” is an observation you can bring to your doctor — it is not, and will never be presented as, a medical conclusion.

This moves Pepa from **passive memory → active assistant**, without pretending to be a physician.

### 8. Quiet Watchfulness

#### Scenario: the shower has been running for twenty minutes.

Pepa doesn’t watch you — **there are no cameras, ever**. It listens to the rhythms of the house.

A shower with someone in it sounds different from a shower running empty. Water running on and on with nobody there is worth a gentle check:

> “The shower has been running unattended for 20 minutes. Is everything okay? Should I notify someone?”
>

The same quiet attention applies to the rhythms of daily life — the kitchen active at breakfast time, doors opening, movement through the rooms. A normal day confirms itself silently. A broken rhythm earns a soft question first, and only then, if needed, a call to someone who cares.

All of it stays home: **sound and simple sensors, processed inside the house, seen by no one else.** This is presence without surveillance — the difference between being watched and being watched over.

# The Big Picture

Most AI assistants today focus on **conversation**.

Pepa focuses on **memory and context**.

The real value is:

**Pepa remembers your life’s digital traces and helps you retrieve meaning from them.**

---

# One Last Thought

The most powerful use of Pepa may not be **questions you ask**, but **things Pepa reminds you about** that you forgot existed.

Example:

> “You saved this article 9 months ago about deterministic AI orchestration. It might be relevant to your Pepa architecture work today.”
>

That’s when Pepa becomes **a cognitive partner rather than just a tool**.
